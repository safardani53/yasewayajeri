We review the Citroen C4 Grand Picasso from price to economy and all its features

February 2024 · 5 minute read

WITH a name like C4 GRAND Picasso you expect Citroen's latest MPV to be larger than life.

But its compact design hides the fact that it's actually a seven-seater, so much so I drove it for a day without even realising and it was only when I opened the boot I discovered the two extra seats.

Top marks ... sleek and stylish C4 Grand Picasso

And if you have children they will just love sitting so far back in the cabin, mine actually argue over who gets to ride way back in the boot and a note to parents, let your two noisiest kids win, then it's quieter up front for you and mum — bonus.

The fact the Picasso looks so good from the outside is one of the strongest points with the Citroen designers scoring top marks and doing a Grand job keeping Picasso dimensions compact.

Road Runner ... cartoon-like rear lights

It's not uncommon for a seven-seater to look nine months pregnant if there has been a dodgy extension slapped onto the rear. The Picasso's rivals, the Toyota Verso, the Kia Carens and Seat Alhambra, are all decent-looking machines, but bulky and bloated to accommodate seven.

But compared to the fat Fiat 500L MPV the Picasso could be easily be described as sexy with it's sleek, svelte body. I love the rear lights, they make me smile every time I look at them as they remind me of a cartoon ACME magnet that you'd see Wiley Coyote using to try and snare Road Runner.. beep, beep. But it's only when you climb inside that you appreciate the amazing space that can only be described as stepping into Dr Who's Tardis.

There's so much room you can stretch out with class-leading cabin space and the most spacious rows two and three in its segment along with maximum leg room — the most accessible row three in segment and the longest row two seat adjustment travel as well.

The massive boot, yip, you guessed it, offers the widest in the category with 1.17m between the wheel arches and a total of 645litres — that's 69litres more than the previous generation, and over 700litres with row two slid forward. Fold all seats flat and you have a massive 2,181litres of space, with a load length of up to 2.75m that's on par with a van.

The Grand Picasso's wheelbase is 2.84m, 11cm longer than the previous generation, increasing cabin and boot capacity but Citroen also managed to reduce front overhangs by 11.6cm, making it the shortest in the class.

Tardis-like ... seats all the family in comfort

Up front, the driver doesn't have a traditional style rev counter and speedo mounted in front of the steering wheel, instead Citroen use a trademark Picasso feature of centrally mounted screens.

On offer now is two large LCD screens, the main 12" display is set deep into the dash and houses all of the car's driving information, speed, revs, fuel, temperature etc.

Then there's a smaller 7" touch screen that uses a similar style approach of button decluttering that Peugeot use with all the main functions being controlled via the screen.

With only a handful of buttons left on the dash, the rest of the controls are saved for the multi-function steering wheel that has no less than 15 buttons.

A cool feature is the ability not just to change the colour of the display but to completely change the theme and layout, similar to what you can do on smart phone.

The colour themes are Elliptic, Cubic plus Graphic and are a brilliant feature but your new themes can be a bit slow to load.

Other smart driver aids are the extra mini rear-view mirror that is perfect for keeping an eye on your meddling kids in the rear and the high-resolution reversing camera.

There are window blinds on the inside of the rear doors, on the back of the front seats there are aeroplane style drop-down tables with cup holders and a flexible strap to hold items secure when driving.

The Grand Picasso is offered with one petrol and two diesel engines.

The petrol engine is a 1.6-litre with either 120bhp, 44.8 combined mpg or a 155bhp version with 46.3mpg. As diesel's will always be the most popular choice for a car of this nature the engine range is more substantial.

Load lugger... van-like storage space

Starting with a 1.6-litre available with 90bhp and an incredible 74.3mpg, then a 115bhp version that provides 70.6mpg.

For more power and performance, there's a 2.0-litre diesel available with 150bhp and 67.3mpg.

My test car was the 1.6-litre, six-speed manual with 115bhp and on the road performance is brisk with a 0-62mph time of 12.1 seconds and a top speed of 117mph.

With 270Nms of torque, there's plenty of low down power when you need it, though to have fun you need to make the most out of the gears.

The six-speed close-ratio gearbox can be a little hard to select, I sometimes found myself in third instead of first.

There are no seven-seater MPVs that handle like a sports car but the C4 does a great job of provided plenty of grip though there's a fair amount of body roll from the front-wheel-drive chassis.

However the ride is smooth and composed and the steering is light and responsive but could do with a bit more feedback through the wheel.

With its futuristic design, the C4 Grand Picasso is not only top of the class it's out of this world.

Key Facts

  • Cost: £19,205 - £27,855
  • Engine: 1.6-litre, four-cylinder turbo diesel
  • Transmission: Six-speed manual, front-wheel drive
  • Power: 115bhp
  • 0-62: 12.6seconds
  • Torque: 270Nms
  • Top Speed: 117mph
  • Economy: 70.6mpg
  • Insurance group: 18E
  • Warranty: 3 year, 60,000 miles
